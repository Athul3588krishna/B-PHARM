Novel Drug Delivery System

NDDS - Advanced Pharmaceutical Delivery Technologies

➢ What is NDDS?

A Novel Drug Delivery System (NDDS) can be defined as a new approach that combines innovative development, formulations, new technologies, and novel methodologies for delivering pharmaceutical compounds in the body to safely achieve the desired pharmacological effects.
➢ NDDS is a system for delivery of drug other than conventional drug delivery system. NDDS is a combination of advanced techniques and new dosage forms which are far better than conventional dosage forms.

➢ The Aim

  • Provide a therapeutic amount of drug to the appropriate site and maintain the desired concentration.
  • Allow the drug to reach the targeted site with minimal side effects.
  • Minimize drug degradation and loss.
  • Increase drug bioavailability.

Classification of NDDS

➢ By Mode of NDDS
  • Targeted drug delivery system
  • Sustained release drug delivery system
  • Controlled release drug delivery system
➢ By Route of Administration
  • Oral drug delivery system
  • Nasopulmonary (pulmonary/inhalation) drug delivery system
  • Mucoadhesive drug delivery system
  • Implantable drug delivery system
  • Other specialized routes

➢ Targeted Drug Delivery System

Targeted drug delivery (smart delivery) increases medication concentration in specific body parts vs others. Approaches include ligand-receptor targeting, antibody-drug conjugates, nanoparticles (liposomes, polymeric NPs), and implant depot systems releasing drug near target tissue.

➢ Sustained Release Drug Delivery System

Systems designed to release medication gradually over an extended period after a single dose to achieve steady therapeutic blood levels (e.g., osmotic pumps, matrix tablets, reservoir systems).

➢ Controlled Release Drug Delivery System

Controlled systems maintain predictable and reproducible release kinetics to keep drug concentrations within a therapeutic window. Examples: polymer matrix systems, coated pellets, biodegradable implants.

➢ Oral Drug Delivery System

The oral route is widely used due to patient familiarity and ease of dosing. Oral NDDS include enteric-coated systems, sustained-release matrix tablets, prodrugs and lipid-based formulations to improve solubility and bioavailability.

➢ Nasopulmonary Drug Delivery System

Pulmonary administration (inhalers, nebulizers) delivers drugs to the lungs for local or systemic effect; advantages include rapid onset and avoidance of first-pass metabolism. Examples: dry powder inhalers (DPIs), metered dose inhalers (MDIs).

➢ Implantable Drug Delivery Systems

Implantables are surgically placed devices (e.g., polymeric implants, osmotic pumps, microchips) that release drug locally or systemically over months to years, minimizing systemic exposure and first-pass loss.

➢ Mucoadhesive Drug Delivery System

Mucoadhesive systems adhere to mucosal tissues (oral, nasal, ocular, vaginal) to prolong residence time, improving local absorption and/or bioavailability (e.g., bioadhesive patches, gels, films).

➢ Advantages & Disadvantages

Advantages

  • Improved patient compliance
  • Accurate dosing
  • Enhanced efficacy and safety
  • Decreased toxicity and side effects
  • Controlled delivery maintaining desired concentration
  • Improved comfort & quality of life
  • Reduced drug accumulation in chronic therapy

Disadvantages

  • Reduced patient acceptance in some cases
  • Repeated dosing may be necessary
  • Higher formulation complexity and cost

Conclusion

Novel drug delivery systems offer significant advantages over conventional formulations by tackling poor solubility, limited bioavailability and systemic toxicity. They deliver drugs more accurately to target sites, reducing off-target effects and improving therapeutic outcomes.
